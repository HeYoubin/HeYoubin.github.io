Featured Research

Wildfire, Ecological Remote Sensing, and Spatially Explainable Fire Modeling

My featured research follows a continuous trajectory from wildfire monitoring and post-fire ecological assessment to spatially explainable fire driver analysis and predictive wildfire modeling. Starting from multi-source remote sensing projects in Xichang City, I investigated burned-area mapping, land-use / land-cover change, and RSEI-based ecological impacts after wildfire disturbance. These experiences motivated my later work on forest and grass fire drivers across Southwest China using spatial statistical and explainable machine learning approaches. They also shaped my current interest in wildfire prediction modeling with multi-source environmental and remote sensing data. Across these projects, I aim to connect ecological remote sensing, GIS, GeoAI, and interpretable spatial modeling for evidence-based fire ecology and environmental risk analysis.

1. Wildfire Monitoring and Burned Area Mapping in Xichang City

Keywords: Wildfire Monitoring; Burned Area Mapping; Sentinel-2; Landsat 8; dNBR; GIS; Xichang City; Ecological Remote Sensing

Overview

This project focuses on forest fire monitoring and burned-area extraction in Xichang City, Sichuan Province. Using multi-source remote sensing imagery, I analyzed pre-fire and post-fire surface conditions, calculated dNBR, and produced GIS-based burned-area maps for the Lushan fire area. The work established my early research foundation in ecological remote sensing and provided the technical basis for later post-fire land-use and ecological assessment.

Data

  • Sentinel-2 imagery
  • Landsat 8 imagery
  • Field survey reference burned area for validation

Methods

  • Pre-fire and post-fire remote sensing image comparison
  • dNBR-based burned-area extraction
  • GIS-based burned-area mapping
  • Area statistics and comparison with a field survey reference

Key Results

  • Estimated approximately 825 ha of burned area.
  • The estimate was within 4% of the 791.6 ha field survey reference.
  • Generated burned-area maps and fire monitoring thematic outputs for the Lushan fire area.

2. Post-fire Land-use / Land-cover Change and RSEI-based Ecological Assessment

Keywords: Land-use / Land-cover Change; RSEI; Post-fire Ecological Assessment; Landsat 8; Sentinel-2; Ecological Remote Sensing; Xichang City

Overview

Building on the burned-area mapping work, this project extends the analysis from fire detection to post-fire landscape and ecological change assessment. It examines land-use / land-cover patterns, built-up expansion, vegetation disturbance, and RSEI-based ecological conditions in Xichang City. The module links remote sensing image preprocessing, supervised classification, land-use transfer analysis, and ecological index mapping to evaluate how wildfire disturbance and regional development were reflected in the landscape.

Data

  • Landsat 8 time-series imagery
  • Sentinel-2 imagery for wildfire-related analysis
  • Reconstructed public-data LULC visualizations for portfolio display
  • RSEI-related remote sensing inputs used for ecological assessment

Methods

  • Supervised land-use / land-cover classification
  • Five land-use categories
  • Land-use change and built-up expansion analysis
  • RSEI-based ecological assessment
  • Transition matrix and land-use transfer visualization for reconstructed portfolio outputs
  • GIS thematic mapping

Key Results

  • Classified five land-use categories using preprocessed Landsat 8 time-series imagery.
  • Quantified 103.5 km² expansion of built-up areas in the original project analysis.
  • Identified a 38.2% decline in "excellent" ecological zones.
  • Observed a 3.3% drop in overall vegetation cover.

3. Spatially Explainable Analysis of Forest and Grass Fire Drivers in Southwest China

Keywords: Forest and Grass Fires; Southwest China; MGWR; GeoShapley; Spatial Explainability; GeoAI; Fire Drivers; Spatial Heterogeneity

Status: Manuscript under review / in revision.

Overview

Building on my earlier work in wildfire monitoring and post-fire ecological assessment, this project extends the research focus from local fire disturbance mapping to regional-scale fire driver analysis. It investigates the spatially heterogeneous drivers of forest and grass fires in Southwest China using an MGWR-GeoShapley framework, aiming to interpret how environmental, climatic, topographic, vegetation, and human-related factors contribute to fire occurrence across space. The project emphasizes mechanism interpretation rather than only prediction, and it connects spatial statistical modeling with explainable GeoAI for wildfire research.

Research Questions

  • What are the dominant driving factors of forest and grass fires in Southwest China?
  • How do fire drivers vary spatially across different ecological and geographic contexts?
  • How can spatial statistical models and explainable AI improve interpretation of wildfire mechanisms?

Data

  • Multi-source geospatial and remote sensing data
  • Fire occurrence / fire event data
  • Meteorological and climatic variables
  • Vegetation and land surface variables
  • Topographic variables
  • Human activity proxies where verified in the manuscript workflow

Methods

  • MGWR for spatially varying relationships
  • GeoShapley and explainable spatial machine learning for interpreting factor contributions
  • GIS-based spatial analysis and visualization

Current Status

  • Manuscript under review / in revision.

4. Wildfire Prediction Modeling in Southwest China

Keywords: Wildfire Prediction; Fire Risk Modeling; XGBoost; Machine Learning; Remote Sensing; Environmental Variables; Model Explainability; Southwest China

Status: Manuscript in preparation.

Overview

This ongoing project further extends my wildfire research from driver interpretation to predictive modeling. Motivated by the question of whether spatially explainable fire mechanisms can support fire risk prediction, the project explores machine learning-based wildfire prediction using multi-source remote sensing, meteorological, topographic, vegetation, and human-related variables in Southwest China. The study aims to evaluate predictive performance while maintaining attention to model interpretability and spatial generalization. Specific performance metrics and final conclusions will be added only after the analysis and manuscript are finalized.

Research Questions

  • Can multi-source geospatial and remote sensing data support reliable wildfire prediction in Southwest China?
  • Which environmental and human-related variables contribute most to predictive performance?
  • How can model interpretability be incorporated into wildfire risk modeling?

Data

  • Multi-source remote sensing and geospatial datasets
  • Meteorological and land surface variables
  • Vegetation and fuel-related indicators
  • Topographic variables
  • Human activity proxies where verified in the modeling workflow

Methods

  • Candidate machine learning models for wildfire prediction
  • XGBoost-based modeling where confirmed by the manuscript workflow
  • Feature engineering and model evaluation
  • Explainable AI analysis where supported by verified results

Current Status

  • Manuscript in preparation.
  • Detailed results will be added after the analysis and manuscript are finalized.
